Why It Matters

Why Resistance Training?

Resistance training — also called strength training or weight training — involves exercises that cause muscles to contract against an external resistance. This resistance can be weights, machines, resistance bands, or even your own bodyweight.

The process of applying resistance causes microscopic tears in muscle fibers. During rest, these fibers repair and grow thicker and stronger — a process called hypertrophy.

"Strength does not come from physical capacity. It comes from an indomitable will."
— Mahatma Gandhi
🔥
Burns Fat
Muscle tissue burns more calories at rest — increasing your basal metabolic rate long-term.
🦴
Bone Density
Weight-bearing exercises stimulate bone growth, reducing osteoporosis risk significantly.
🩺
Health Markers
Improves insulin sensitivity, blood pressure, cholesterol, and reduces inflammation.
🧠
Mental Health
Releases endorphins and improves self-confidence, reducing anxiety and depression symptoms.
Anatomy & Training

Major Muscle Groups

Click any muscle group to see anatomy, function, and the best exercises to target it.

💪 Shoulders
Deltoid Muscles — Anterior, Lateral & Posterior heads
The deltoids are the rounded muscles that form the contour of the shoulder. They are composed of three distinct heads, each responsible for different movements. Well-developed deltoids create the "wide shoulder" look and protect the shoulder joint from injury.
Primary Functions
  • Arm abduction (lifting arm to the side)
  • Arm flexion (lifting arm forward)
  • Arm extension (pulling arm backward)
  • Internal and external rotation of the arm
Best Exercises
Overhead Press (OHP)
King of shoulder exercises — hits all 3 heads
3–4 × 6–10
Lateral Raises
Isolates the lateral head — builds width
3–4 × 12–15
Front Raises
Targets anterior deltoid
3 × 12
Reverse Flyes
Posterior deltoid — often neglected
3 × 12–15
Arnold Press
Rotational press — hits all heads uniquely
3 × 8–12
🫀 Chest
Pectoralis Major & Pectoralis Minor
The pectorals are the large fan-shaped muscles of the chest. Pectoralis major is the primary mover for pushing movements and arm adduction. The pectoralis minor lies beneath it, helping stabilize the shoulder blade. A strong chest contributes to better posture and upper body power.
Primary Functions
  • Horizontal pushing (pressing away from body)
  • Arm adduction (bringing arms toward chest)
  • Internal rotation of the shoulder
  • Assists in deep breathing (pec minor)
Best Exercises
Barbell Bench Press
The foundational chest exercise
3–5 × 5–8
Push-Ups
Bodyweight classic — scalable for all levels
3 × Max
Incline Dumbbell Press
Targets upper chest for full development
3–4 × 8–12
Cable/Dumbbell Flyes
Peak contraction — great for muscle shaping
3 × 12–15
Dips
Compound movement hitting lower chest
3 × 8–12
🔺 Back
Latissimus Dorsi, Trapezius & Rhomboids
The back is the largest muscle complex of the upper body. Latissimus Dorsi (Lats) are the broad muscles creating the V-taper. The Trapezius (Traps) runs from the neck down the middle back. Rhomboids sit between the shoulder blades. A strong back is essential for posture, injury prevention, and overall athletic performance.
Primary Functions
  • Pulling arm toward the body (lat pulldown, rows)
  • Shoulder elevation and depression (traps)
  • Scapular retraction — pulling shoulders back
  • Spinal extension and stabilization
Best Exercises
Pull-Ups / Chin-Ups
Best lat builder — bodyweight or weighted
3–4 × 5–10
Barbell Bent-Over Row
Thickness builder — hits all back muscles
3–4 × 6–10
Lat Pulldown
Pull-up alternative — great for beginners
3 × 10–12
Seated Cable Row
Mid-back focus — improves posture
3 × 10–12
Deadlift
King of all exercises — full posterior chain
3–5 × 3–5
🦾 Biceps
Biceps Brachii — Long head & Short head
The biceps brachii has two heads — long (outer, creates the "peak") and short (inner, adds width). The brachialis lies underneath and contributes significantly to arm thickness. Contrary to popular belief, biceps make up only about 1/3 of the upper arm — triceps make up 2/3.
Primary Functions
  • Elbow flexion (curling motion)
  • Forearm supination (rotating palm upward)
  • Assists in shoulder flexion
Best Exercises
Barbell Curl
Mass builder — allows heaviest load
3–4 × 8–12
Hammer Curls
Hits brachialis and brachioradialis
3 × 10–12
Incline Dumbbell Curl
Long head stretch — great for peak
3 × 10–12
Preacher Curl
Eliminates cheating — pure isolation
3 × 12
Chin-Ups
Compound — biceps + back, best combo
3 × Max
⚡ Triceps
Triceps Brachii — Long, Lateral & Medial heads
The triceps make up approximately 2/3 of the upper arm mass — making them more important than biceps for arm size. The long head is the largest and most visible from behind. The lateral head creates the horseshoe shape visible on the side. Training all three heads ensures complete development.
Primary Functions
  • Elbow extension (straightening the arm)
  • Assists in shoulder extension (long head)
  • All pushing movements involve triceps
Best Exercises
Close-Grip Bench Press
Heavy compound — great for overall mass
3–4 × 6–10
Skull Crushers (EZ Bar)
Long head emphasis — great mass builder
3 × 8–12
Tricep Rope Pushdown
Lateral head — creates horseshoe shape
3–4 × 12–15
Overhead Tricep Extension
Best long head stretch — adds thickness
3 × 10–12
Dips
Bodyweight compound — all 3 heads
3 × Max
🦵 Quadriceps
Rectus Femoris, Vastus Lateralis, Medialis & Intermedius
The quadriceps are the four muscles on the front of the thigh — the largest muscle group in the body. They are the primary movers in squatting and leg extension. Strong quads protect the knee joint, improve athletic performance, and are critical for everyday movements like walking, climbing stairs, and standing up.
Primary Functions
  • Knee extension (straightening the leg)
  • Hip flexion (rectus femoris only)
  • Stabilization during squatting and lunging
Best Exercises
Barbell Back Squat
King of leg exercises — full lower body
3–5 × 5–8
Leg Press
Quad-dominant — safer for beginners
3–4 × 10–15
Lunges
Unilateral — fixes muscle imbalances
3 × 10 each
Bulgarian Split Squat
Best single-leg exercise for quads
3 × 8 each
Leg Extension
Isolation — great for VMO development
3 × 12–15
🍑 Glutes
Gluteus Maximus, Medius & Minimus
The gluteal muscles are the largest and most powerful muscles in the human body. Gluteus Maximus is responsible for hip extension and external rotation. Gluteus Medius abducts the hip and stabilizes the pelvis during walking. Strong glutes are essential for athletic performance, lower back health, and overall power.
Primary Functions
  • Hip extension (driving hips forward)
  • Hip abduction (moving leg outward)
  • External rotation of the hip
  • Pelvic stabilization during movement
Best Exercises
Hip Thrusts
Best glute activator — must include
3–4 × 10–15
Deadlift
Massive glute + hamstring recruiter
3–5 × 3–6
Romanian Deadlift
Hip hinge — glutes and hamstrings
3 × 8–12
Glute Bridges
Bodyweight version of hip thrust
3 × 15–20
Cable Kickbacks
Isolation — maximus contraction
3 × 15 each
🦿 Hamstrings
Biceps Femoris, Semitendinosus & Semimembranosus
The hamstrings are three muscles on the back of the thigh. They work opposite the quadriceps and are the most commonly injured muscle group in athletes — usually due to being undertrained relative to the quads. Strong, flexible hamstrings prevent knee and lower back injuries.
Primary Functions
  • Knee flexion (curling the leg)
  • Hip extension (assists glutes)
  • Pelvic posterior tilt
  • Deceleration during running
Best Exercises
Romanian Deadlift
Best hamstring mass and strength builder
3–4 × 8–12
Lying Leg Curl
Isolation — peak contraction at short length
3–4 × 10–15
Nordic Hamstring Curl
Elite exercise for injury prevention
3 × 5–8
Seated Leg Curl
Long head emphasis — great complement
3 × 10–12
Good Mornings
Hip hinge — builds hamstring strength
3 × 10
Unit IV

Training Variables

Manipulating these six variables is how you design a program that produces results and avoids plateaus.

📊
Intensity
How heavy you lift, expressed as % of your 1 Rep Max (1RM). Higher intensity = lower reps. Lower intensity = higher reps.
Strength: 80–90% 1RM · Hypertrophy: 65–80% 1RM
🔁
Repetitions (Reps)
Number of times you perform a movement in a set. Different rep ranges target different adaptations.
1–5 Strength · 6–12 Hypertrophy · 12+ Endurance
📈
Volume
Total work done — sets × reps × weight. Progressive increase in volume over weeks drives muscle growth.
Beginner: 10–15 sets/muscle/week · Advanced: 16–22
⏱️
Tempo
Speed of each phase of the rep — eccentric (lowering), pause, concentric (lifting). Slower tempos increase time under tension.
3-1-1 = 3s down, 1s pause, 1s up
📅
Frequency
How often you train each muscle group per week. Research shows training each muscle 2x/week is optimal for most people.
Beginner: 3 days · Intermediate: 4–5 · Advanced: 6
💤
Rest Period
Time between sets. Short rest = more metabolic stress. Long rest = more strength. Choose based on your goal.
Strength: 2–5 min · Hypertrophy: 60–90 sec
Training Levels

Beginner to Advanced

A structured progression that takes you from your first gym session to elite-level training.

🌱 Level 1
Beginner Plan
  • 3 days/week, full body each session
  • 2–3 sets per exercise, 10–15 reps
  • Focus: learning movement patterns
  • Compound lifts only (squat, press, row)
  • 2 min rest between sets
  • Duration: First 3–6 months
Squat · Press · Row · Hinge · Carry
🌿 Level 2
Intermediate Plan
  • 4 days/week, upper/lower split
  • 3–4 sets, mixed rep ranges 6–15
  • Introduce isolation exercises
  • Progressive overload tracked weekly
  • 90 sec – 2 min rest
  • Duration: Months 6–24
Upper/Lower · PPL · Periodization
🌳 Level 3
Advanced Plan
  • 5–6 days/week, body-part splits
  • 4–5 sets, 3–20 reps (varied)
  • Periodization — deload weeks
  • Accessory work for weak points
  • Tempo manipulation + drop sets
  • Duration: Year 2+ onwards
PPL · Bro Split · Conjugate Method
