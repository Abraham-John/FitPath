Definition

What is Exercise?

Exercise is a subset of physical activity that is planned, structured, repetitive, and has as a final or intermediate objective the improvement or maintenance of one or more components of physical fitness.

Unlike incidental movement, exercise is intentional. Its benefits go far beyond weight management — it improves cardiovascular health, mental wellbeing, hormonal balance, bone density, and longevity.

Regular exercise improves cognitive function, memory, and academic performance. It reduces stress and anxiety — major concerns for students. Even 30 minutes of moderate activity daily improves focus, sleep quality, and mood through the release of endorphins and neurotrophic factors in the brain.
WHO recommends at least 150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week (or 75–150 minutes of vigorous), plus muscle-strengthening activities (resistance training) on 2 or more days per week targeting all major muscle groups.
Physical activity is any body movement produced by skeletal muscles that requires energy (walking, climbing stairs, household chores). Exercise is planned, structured, repetitive physical activity with a specific fitness goal. All exercise is physical activity, but not all physical activity is exercise.

Exercise Classification

Types of Exercise

Different types of exercise target different systems of the body. A well-rounded routine includes elements of all types.

🏃
Aerobic / Cardio
Sustained, rhythmic exercise that uses oxygen to fuel the working muscles. Improves cardiovascular endurance and burns significant calories.
RunningCyclingSwimmingJump Rope
💪
Anaerobic / Resistance
High-intensity exercise performed without oxygen — short bursts that build strength, power, and muscle mass. Elevates metabolism long after the session.
Weight LiftingSprintingHIIT
🧘
Flexibility
Stretching and mobility work that improves joint range of motion, reduces injury risk, and aids recovery. Critical but often neglected.
YogaStatic StretchFoam Rolling
⚖️
Balance & Stability
Exercises that challenge the body's equilibrium, improving coordination, proprioception, and core strength. Reduces fall risk, especially important with age.
Balance BoardSingle-leg DrillsPilates
🏋️
Functional Training
Movements that mimic real-life activities, improving the way you move daily. Reduces injury risk and improves overall athletic performance.
SquatsDeadliftsCarries
🔄
HIIT
High-Intensity Interval Training — alternating short bursts of intense exercise with brief rest periods. Maximally efficient for fat loss and cardio improvement.
TabataCircuit TrainingSprint Intervals
The 5 Pillars

Components of Fitness

True fitness is multi-dimensional. These five components together define what it means to be physically fit.

❤️
Cardiovascular Endurance
The heart's ability to efficiently deliver oxygen to working muscles during sustained exercise.
💪
Muscular Strength
The maximum amount of force a muscle or group of muscles can exert against resistance in a single effort.
🔄
Muscular Endurance
The ability of muscles to repeatedly exert force over time without fatiguing — important for daily activities.
🤸
Flexibility
The range of motion available at a joint. Good flexibility reduces injury risk and improves posture and movement quality.
⚖️
Body Composition
The ratio of lean body mass (muscle, bone, organs) to fat mass. A healthy composition improves all health markers.
Exercise Programming

The FITT Principle

Four variables that define any exercise program. Adjust them to progress, prevent plateaus, and recover properly.

F
Frequency
How often you train per week. Beginners: 3 days. Intermediate: 4–5 days. Advanced: 5–6 days.
I
Intensity
How hard you train. Measured by heart rate, RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion), or percentage of 1 Rep Max.
T
Time
Duration of each session. 30–60 min is optimal for most goals. Longer is not always better.
T
Type
The kind of exercise — cardio, resistance, flexibility, or a combination. Choose based on your goals.
Prescriptive Exercise

Exercise for Lifestyle Disorders

Specific exercise recommendations for common lifestyle disorders — backed by clinical evidence.

🩸
Type 2 Diabetes
  • 150 min/week moderate aerobic exercise
  • Resistance training 2–3x/week
  • Walk for 10 min after each meal
  • Avoid prolonged sitting — break every 30 min
⚖️
Obesity
  • 300+ min/week of moderate aerobic activity
  • Resistance training to preserve muscle
  • Progressive increase in volume over time
  • NEAT (non-exercise activity) is equally important
🌸
PCOS
  • Moderate intensity cardio 3–4x/week
  • Yoga and Pilates for hormone regulation
  • Resistance training improves insulin sensitivity
  • Avoid excessive high-intensity training (raises cortisol)
💔
Metabolic Syndrome
  • Aerobic exercise 5 days/week, 30 min each
  • Strength training to improve glucose uptake
  • Target 5–10% body weight reduction
  • Consistency over intensity — make it a habit
